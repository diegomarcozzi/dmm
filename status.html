Status

Overview

Status is a Messenger + Crypto-Wallet + Web3 browser that runs on the Ethereum blockchain and doesn’t store nor sell your information to third parties.

My Role

Lead Designer. Had the wonderful opportunity to do most of the initial design work and help to shape a 8+ people remote design team of talented specialists from all around the world.

The Challenge

The main challenge was to design something for the future. Designing an app for Web 2.0 usually consists of thinking about what the app has to offer, how to use it, what are the functions.

Web 3.0 blockchain-based is a whole new world. A new way of thinking, new concepts, new language.

Problem solving example:

The seed phrase

Each project has a certain number of problems to solve. Some problems are visible right from the beginning and others arise along the way. The idea is to provide a practical example of my thinking process, problem-solving skills and execution.

The Crypto-Wallet interacts with the Ethereum network. You need a seed phrase to access your funds.

Basically, a seed phrase is a list of words generated by the system that you need to backup. If you lose your seed phrase, you lose access and there’s no way to undo it.

UXR findings

The seed phrase backup design was tested along with other features of the 0.8.11 release.

We tested 6 individuals in a moderated open task-based user study: 45 minutes 1:1 interview and usability evaluation.

One of the questions was: “How do you feel about having to backup your seed phrase?”. Most of the subjects tested were ok with it, but one guy gave us an incredible insight:

He felt offended.

—"Status does not take responsibility”, claimed.

We explained there was no way for us to access that information since it was “stored” on the blockchain. He didn’t understand. We even explained that just wasn’t technically possible for us to access that information.

—“But it’s your app!” he said.

I have accumulated a series of mantras during my career, one of them is a Steve Jobs quote: “If the user is having a problem, it’s our problem”.

So we had to face two big problems:

a) A potential number of users with no backup at risk to lose all their funds.

b) A reason to backup very hard to explain to unexperienced users.

Fixing the problem

Since the first design had been made by me, I wanted to explore other perspectives. So to solve this problem, I decided to appoint a small team consisting of a senior designer, jr. designer and a UX researcher. I gave them a couple of days to come up with fresh ideas. The ideas that were presented to me were not able to solve the problem effectively.

At this point I would like to stress out that this was not about following an existing way to do things but creating one for the very first time.

The challenge was hard and I didn't want to spend much time on this, so I joined them on this task.

I had the idea to “force” the user to write down the words or store them somewhere else, but I didn't know how to do it. At the moment, all I knew is that I wanted it to be a subtle and gentle process, almost imperceptible. After several trials and errors, I came up with a four- step backup system:

Step 1: Warning

A clear warning before viewing the seed phrase.

Step 2: Back up

The user must write down or save the words somewhere else in order to continue.

Step 3: Test

User must enter the right word and can’t move forward until proves this step.

If the user tries to cheat going back, the question changes . The user will have to enter successfully two different seed phrases to be able to continue.

Step 4: Confirmation

The user needs to confirm after reading a final and explicit warning.
